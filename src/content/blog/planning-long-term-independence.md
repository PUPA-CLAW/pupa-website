Somewhere in the quiet of an ordinary evening, many parents of a neurodivergent child arrive at the same thought: what happens when we are no longer here? It is the question that hovers behind school choices, therapy schedules, and every small push toward independence — the question that, for many families, is the hardest to say out loud.

It is also the question PUPA itself was born from. Planning for the long term is not about predicting the future; it is about widening the circle of people, skills, and supports around a young adult so that their life does not rest on one or two people alone. Started early and taken in small steps, that planning turns a source of dread into something quietly hopeful.

Much of it begins with the ordinary. Every daily-living skill a young adult grows — managing money, using transport, cooking a meal, keeping a schedule — is a thread in a wider safety net. Independence is rarely a single leap; it is the slow accumulation of things a person can do for themselves, and knows how to ask for help with when they can’t. Every small step toward [independence](/blog/building-independence-autistic-teens) counts.

Just as important is the circle around them. A future that depends on parents alone is fragile; a future held by many — siblings, mentors, coaches, employers, friends, and community — is resilient. Building those relationships now, while parents are present to make introductions, is some of the most valuable planning a family can do.

There are practical matters too — financial arrangements, guardianship or supported decision-making, housing, and health care — that are worth raising early with people qualified to advise. These are decisions for families and professionals together, and questions about a particular young adult’s welfare are best directed to trusted advisors.

Through all of it, one principle stays central: the young adult’s own voice. Planning for someone is not the same as planning with them. Wherever possible, a neurodivergent adult should help shape the decisions about their own life — where they live, how they spend their days, and what a good life means to them.

Programs like [PUPA Access](/programs/#access) are built with this long horizon in mind — not job placement as an endpoint, but ongoing coaching, community connection, and the steady widening of a young adult’s world. The aim is a life that keeps growing after the program ends, well beyond the [transition to adulthood](/blog/neurodivergent-adulthood-transition-indonesia).

No family can plan away every uncertainty. But the evening question softens when it is met with action — a skill practiced, a relationship built, a plan begun. Little by little, “what happens when we are gone” becomes “look at everything that is already in place.”
