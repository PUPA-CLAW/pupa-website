There is a question that often arrives quietly, almost in a whisper, from parents of neurodivergent young adults: will my child ever have a partner? It is rarely asked in formal settings. It surfaces in the margins of conversations, carrying a mixture of hope and worry — and, very often, of simply not knowing where to start.

Yet the answer, study after study suggests, begins with a simple fact: the desire to love and be loved is real. Qualitative research with autistic adolescents and adults published in Sexuality and Disability (2024) found that most young autistic people are interested in romantic relationships — just like their neurotypical peers. A comprehensive scoping review in Autism in Adulthood (2025) of autistic adults' relationship preferences confirms it: what differs is not the wanting, but the way it is experienced and the support that is available.

## The same desire, unequal preparation

This is where the gap opens. Research with neurodivergent young people in the UK (2024) found a repeating pattern: interest in romantic relationships is high, but the relationship and sexuality education they receive is rarely designed for how they understand the world. Material that is overly implicit, full of unexplained social cues, leaves many autistic young adults learning about love from the riskiest sources: trial and error, the unguided internet, or nothing at all.

The consequences can be serious. Without preparation, neurodivergent young adults are more vulnerable to misunderstanding, wounding rejection, even unsafe or exploitative relationships. Conversely, comparative research in the Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders (2024) shows that with the right understanding and support, autistic people can build warm, mutually respectful relationships — in their own way, without having to copy the neurotypical "script."

Some parents will also discover that their child's path in love is not the one they imagined. Recent research documents the diversity of orientation and identity among autistic people. Whatever the form, the principles of support are the same: respect, safety, and honesty.

## Start with small conversations

So where does a family begin? Not with one awkward "big talk," but with small, repeated, open conversations.

First, make the topic speakable. Many neurodivergent young adults do not ask — not because they don't want to know, but because they have picked up the signal that the topic is off-limits. A sentence as simple as "if you like someone, you can tell us" opens that door.

Second, teach consent explicitly. Precisely because social cues are often implicit, clear rules help enormously: ask before touching, accept "no" without pushing, and know that they too have the right to say "no." This is not only about romance — it is a personal safety skill.

Third, use concrete ways of learning. Real scenarios, example sentences, discussing scenes from a film together — all of it helps more than abstract advice like "just be polite."

Fourth, talk about heartbreak too. Being turned down, misreading a situation, breaking up — these are part of learning to love. A young adult who knows heartbreak is allowed to be felt, and can be talked about, recovers in a healthier way.

At PUPA, conversations about relationships do not stand alone; they are part of the broader life skills participants build step by step — understanding themselves, reading social situations, and voicing their needs — always at each person's own pace and readiness.

## The right to love

In the end, this is not about making sure everyone marries or pairs off. Some will choose a different life, and that too deserves respect. It is about something more fundamental: making sure every neurodivergent young adult knows they are worthy of love, capable of loving respectfully, and never alone with the most human of feelings.

May the question once whispered anxiously one day be asked calmly — not "could my child ever be loved?" but "how do I walk with them as they learn to love?"
