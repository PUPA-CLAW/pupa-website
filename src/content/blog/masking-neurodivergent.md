At school, at an internship, or at a big family gathering, they are known as polite, calm, someone who "doesn't look autistic." They hold eye contact even when it is uncomfortable, still the hand movements that soothe them, laugh at the right moments, and answer small talk from a script rehearsed in private. Then the front door closes — and everything runs out. The rest of the day passes in silence, exhaustion, sometimes tears that cannot be explained.

For many families the scene is bewildering: weren't they fine just now, out there? Research calls that invisible effort masking, or social camouflaging — concealing one's natural ways of thinking, feeling, and moving to match what the environment expects. And its price, according to current science, is far higher than it looks.

## The effort no one sees

Camouflaging takes many forms: suppressing stimming (the repetitive movements that calm a person), forcing eye contact, imitating others' speech styles, memorizing conversation "scripts," or hiding sensory discomfort in loud, bright places. At a glance it can look like adaptability. The problem is that it runs continuously, without pause, on full conscious effort — like performing a role all day, every day.

Studies of autistic adults find a consistent link between camouflaging and deep exhaustion and depressive symptoms (Autism in Adulthood, 2025). A 2026 systematic review in Research in Neurodiversity concluded that camouflaging and autistic burnout are tightly intertwined — [the subject of our January article](/blog/autistic-burnout). In other words: the longer and more tightly the mask is worn, the more it drains.

There is also a cultural dimension worth reflecting on. A 2026 study in the journal Autism of Latino autistic young adults found that cultural values — expectations of harmony, politeness, and protecting the family's good name — can intensify the pressure to camouflage. The finding lands close to home in many collectivist cultures, where "sparing others' feelings" and "not causing embarrassment" are taught from childhood. Our culture's good intentions can, without meaning to, add weight to the masks our children wear.

Camouflaging is also one reason many neurodivergent people — especially women — are only recognized in adulthood. Those most skilled at blending in are most often overlooked, and become "visible" only when their energy is entirely spent.

## A home without masks

If camouflaging grows from feeling unsafe to be oneself, the remedy points in a clear direction: safety. And the first place to build it is home.

A home without masks is not a home without rules. It is a home where stimming is not scolded, eye contact is not forced, the need to withdraw after social events is respected, and the sentence "I'm tired of pretending today" is met with a hug, not a lecture. The research on burnout and camouflaging shows that spaces like these are where [recovery becomes possible](/blog/emotional-regulation-neurodivergent-young-adults).

Families can also help by changing the question. Instead of "why can't you behave like you did in front of the guests?", try "what tired you out the most today?". The first question asks for the mask to go back on; the second gives permission to take it off.

At PUPA, this principle shapes everyday practice: participants are not asked to appear neurotypical, but supported to understand themselves and [voice their needs](/blog/self-advocacy-neurodivergent-young-adults). Simply being among peers who do not need to pretend to one another — that alone restores.

## Toward an accepting April

Next month, the world marks Autism Acceptance Month. The key word is no longer just "awareness" but "acceptance" — and this article is the reason why. As long as the world only welcomes neurodivergent people who successfully disguise themselves, the mask will stay on, and its price will keep being paid by the youngest.

Real acceptance is measured by something simple: how safe a person feels to be themselves in front of us. At home, at school, at work — may the answer, more and more often, be: safe.
