Every family with an autistic member usually has its own version of the story. One knows the route and number of every bus in the city. One has drawn the same character hundreds of times until the folds of the clothing are perfect. One can explain dinosaur lineages, train timetables, or the history of airline logos with a curator's precision. And nearly every family has heard the same comment from people around them: "always the same thing?"

For decades, the world — including the professional world — treated such special interests as symptoms to be reduced. Recent research has turned that view almost completely around.

## Not a symptom, but a door

A study in Autism in Adulthood (2025) tracing autistic people's own narratives found the right name for the role these interests play: access points. A special interest, the research shows, is not merely a pastime; it is how an autistic person makes sense of the world, regulates themselves, recovers energy, and builds identity. A 2026 systematic review of how autistic people experience their strengths found the same: deep interests are a source of joy, confidence, and — when they meet the right field — genuine excellence.

Qualitative research in the journal Autism (2024) added a striking finding: special interests can act as both a social "boundary" and a "bridge." A boundary, when the surroundings don't understand them. A bridge, when they connect a person to a community that shares the same passion — fellow train lovers, illustrators, programmers, cooks.

The world of work is beginning to read the same direction. A scoping review of autistic people's career progression in Autism (2024) urges a shift in mindset: from forcing people to fit any job, toward matching jobs to a person's strengths and interests. Interest-based strengths programs — like the STEAM program studied in the Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders (2024) — showed effects on confidence and on how autistic teenagers see themselves. That study's title comes from a parent's words about his son: "He sees his autism as a strength, not a deficit now."

## Accompany, don't extinguish

Parents' worries remain valid, of course. What if the interest takes over all their time? What if they refuse everything else? Research offers an answer that is both reassuring and honest: the problem is almost never the interest itself, but the balance of life around it. And balance is far easier to build by working with the interest than against it.

A few practical ways to begin at home.

Enter their world first. Ask, listen, let them teach you. For many autistic people, sharing an interest is the most sincere form of closeness — and the best doorway into every other conversation.

Use the interest as an anchor for learning. Arithmetic through train timetables, reading through the dinosaur encyclopedia, English through a favorite game. New skills stick best to an interest that is already burning.

Build the bridge. Find the community, class, or competition that connects them with people who share the passion. Friendships often grow more easily there than at ordinary social events.

Guide it gently toward craft. Drawings become a portfolio. Memorized routes become data precision. A love of cooking becomes kitchen skill. At PUPA Karya, this is precisely the work: each participant's interest becomes the starting point for building craft skills and work readiness — step by step, with pride.

## A flame worth guarding

Not every interest will become a profession — nor does it need to. Some will remain a place to come home to: a source of calm on hard days, a language of friendship, a small burning pride. That alone is more than enough.

Only one thing truly matters: don't put out the flame. The world already asks neurodivergent people to make themselves smaller far too often. At home, let the interest be kept alight — because from that small flame, many doors open.
