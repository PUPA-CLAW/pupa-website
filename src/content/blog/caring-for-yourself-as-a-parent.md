Throughout this year, we have written a great deal about neurodivergent young adults — [independence](/blog/building-independence-autistic-teens), [life skills](/blog/life-skills-autistic-young-adults), [work](/blog/vocational-readiness-neurodivergent-young-adults), [emotions](/blog/emotional-regulation-neurodivergent-young-adults), and [social connection](/blog/friendship-social-connection-neurodivergent-young-adults). As the year closes, allow us to write about someone rarely written about: you, the parent.

Walking with a child toward adulthood is a long journey for any family. For families of autistic and neurodivergent young adults, that journey is often longer, holds more uncertainty, and the role of supporting does not end at eighteen.

International research shows that parents of neurodivergent individuals often carry higher and longer-lasting stress than other parents — and that a parent's wellbeing is closely tied to the quality of support they are able to give. Caring for yourself is not selfishness. It is part of caring for your child.

So at the end of this year, there are a few things we want to say to you, sincerely.

You are allowed to be tired. Tiredness does not mean less love. Acknowledging it is the first step toward recovering — and your child learns about emotional regulation partly by watching you look after yourself.

You are allowed to share the load. Support does not have to rest on one pair of shoulders. Involving a partner, siblings, extended family, or community — however small their part — makes this journey more sustainable. Building that circle of support now is also part of the long-term planning that matters for your child's future.

You are not alone. Many families find strength in meeting other families who understand without needing explanations. A simple conversation with a fellow parent often restores more than any advice. At PUPA, we see every day how families strengthen one another — and we believe families are partners, not spectators, in every participant's journey.

Celebrate the small steps. Progress is not always visible from the outside. Ordering their own meal, trying a new route, staying through a crowded activity — each is the fruit of your child's hard work and your patience. Small steps, celebrated, grow into confidence.

And finally: hope is well founded. With the right support, accepting surroundings, and enough time, autistic and neurodivergent young adults keep growing throughout their lives. There is no age limit on learning, and no single road to a life that matters.

Thank you for walking with us this year. We will see you next year — with new stories, new steps, and new growth.
